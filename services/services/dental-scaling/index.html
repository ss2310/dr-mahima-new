"Scaling Damages Teeth" — The Myth That Costs Lives

This is the most dangerous dental myth in India. Let's address it head-on.

The Myth: "Scaling loosens teeth and creates gaps"

The truth: Tartar builds up between and around your teeth over months and years. It acts like a cement — filling gaps and holding loose teeth together. When scaling removes the tartar, patients see the gaps and feel slight looseness and wrongly blame the cleaning.

What's actually happening: Those gaps were always there — they were just hidden by tartar. The looseness was already there — the tartar was acting as a false splint while the disease continued destroying bone underneath. The tartar itself was causing the bone loss and loosening. Removing it is the first step in stopping the damage.

The analogy: Blaming scaling for loose teeth is like blaming the doctor for finding cancer during a checkup. The disease was already there — the dentist just uncovered it. If you don't remove the tartar, the gum disease continues silently until your teeth fall out on their own.

What Professional Scaling Actually Does for You

Removes Tartar (Calculus)

Plaque (soft bacterial film) hardens into tartar within 24–48 hours. Once hardened, tartar cannot be removed by brushing or flossing — only professional scaling can remove it. Tartar harbors billions of bacteria that produce toxins, destroying gum tissue and bone.

Prevents Gum Disease

Gum disease (periodontitis) is the #1 cause of tooth loss in adults over 35 — not cavities. It destroys the bone holding your teeth silently and painlessly until teeth become loose. Regular scaling every 6 months prevents this entirely. It is the single most important preventive dental procedure.

Removes Stains

Tea, coffee, tobacco, and pan/gutka leave stubborn stains that brushing alone cannot remove. Professional polishing after scaling removes these surface stains, making teeth visibly cleaner and brighter. This is not the same as whitening (which changes the internal tooth color), but the improvement is noticeable.

Freshens Breath

Chronic bad breath (halitosis) is most commonly caused by tartar buildup and gum disease — bacteria produce foul-smelling sulfur compounds. Mouthwash only masks the smell temporarily. Scaling removes the source of the odor. If your bad breath persists despite brushing, scaling is likely what you need.

What Happens During a Scaling Appointment?

Step 1: Examination (5 min)

We check your teeth and gums — looking for tartar deposits, gum recession, pocket depth, bleeding, and signs of gum disease. This determines whether standard scaling is enough or deep scaling is needed.

Step 2: Ultrasonic Scaling (15–25 min)

An ultrasonic scaler tip vibrates at high frequency, breaking up tartar deposits with minimal contact. A water spray flushes debris and keeps the area cool. You'll feel vibration and cold water — mild discomfort, not pain. For sensitive patients, topical numbing gel is available.

Step 3: Hand Scaling (5–10 min)

After ultrasonic scaling removes the bulk of tartar, hand instruments (curettes) are used for precision cleaning in tight areas — between teeth, under the gum line, and around dental restorations. This ensures no deposits are left behind.

Step 4: Polishing (5 min)

A rotating rubber cup with prophylaxis paste is used to polish all tooth surfaces. This removes surface stains from tea, coffee, and tobacco, and smooths the tooth surface so plaque is less likely to adhere. Your teeth feel noticeably smooth and clean afterward.

Signs You Need Professional Scaling Now

Visible Signs

Yellow or brown deposits on the back of your lower front teeth (the most common tartar spot). Dark buildup along the gum line. Teeth that look longer than before (gum recession). Visible gaps that weren't there before. Red, puffy gums instead of firm, pink ones. Brown stains from tea, coffee, or tobacco that don't brush off.

Symptoms You Feel

Gums bleed when you brush or floss. Persistent bad breath that doesn't go away with brushing or mouthwash. A bad taste in your mouth. Teeth feeling rough when you run your tongue over them. Gums that are tender or sore. Teeth feeling slightly loose. Food getting stuck between teeth more than before.

Even if you have none of these signs, get scaling every 6 months. Gum disease is painless in its early stages — by the time you feel symptoms, significant damage has already occurred.

After Scaling — What to Expect

First 1–3 Days

Sensitivity: Mild sensitivity to hot and cold is normal — use desensitizing toothpaste (Sensodyne). Slight bleeding: Gums may bleed slightly for 1–2 days during brushing — this stops as gums heal. Gaps: You may notice small gaps between teeth that were previously filled with tartar — this is normal, the gaps were always there. Continue brushing and flossing normally.

Long-Term Maintenance

Brush twice daily — morning and before bed (bedtime brush is most important). Floss daily — brushing alone misses 40% of tooth surfaces. Use fluoride toothpaste — strengthens enamel. Schedule scaling every 6 months — tartar starts reforming within weeks, regular cleaning prevents buildup. Reduce tea, coffee, tobacco — these accelerate staining and tartar formation.
