When Does a Tooth Need to Be Extracted?

Extraction is always the last resort. We try to save every tooth first. But sometimes extraction is the right choice.

Extraction Is Needed When:

The tooth is too damaged by decay to be saved — even a root canal won't help. Severe infection with abscess that doesn't respond to antibiotics. The tooth is fractured below the gum line. Advanced gum disease has made the tooth very loose. Impacted wisdom tooth is causing pain, infection, or damage to the next tooth. Orthodontic treatment (braces) requires space creation. A baby tooth is not falling out and is blocking the permanent tooth.

Extraction Is NOT Needed When:

The tooth can be saved with a filling — even a large one. A root canal can remove the infection and save the tooth. The tooth is slightly loose but gum treatment can stabilize it. A wisdom tooth is erupted, positioned correctly, and not causing problems. Pain can be resolved with treatment other than removal. If a dentist recommends extraction without showing you the X-ray and explaining why the tooth can't be saved — get a second opinion.

What Happens During a Tooth Extraction?

Step 1: X-Ray & Assessment (5 min)

We take a dental X-ray to see the full root structure, bone around the tooth, and proximity to nerves. This tells us whether a simple or surgical approach is needed. We show you the X-ray and explain the plan.

Step 2: Numbing (2–3 min)

Local anesthesia is administered to numb the tooth and surrounding area completely. We apply topical numbing gel before the injection so you don't feel the needle. Within 2–3 minutes, the area is fully numb — you feel zero pain.

Step 3: Loosening & Removal (5–30 min)

Simple extraction: The tooth is gently loosened with an elevator instrument and removed with forceps. Takes 5–10 minutes. You feel pressure but no pain. Surgical extraction: A small incision in the gum is made. Sometimes the tooth is sectioned (cut) into pieces for easier removal. Sutures (stitches) close the site. Takes 20–30 minutes.

Step 4: Post-Extraction Care

We place a gauze pad on the extraction site — bite firmly for 30–45 minutes. We prescribe painkillers (Paracetamol) and antibiotics if needed. You receive detailed aftercare instructions. If sutures were placed, a follow-up visit is scheduled in 7 days for removal.

After Extraction — Critical Do's & Don'ts

Following these instructions prevents dry socket and ensures proper healing.

✓ Do

Bite firmly on gauze for 30–45 minutes. Take prescribed medications on time. Eat soft, cool foods (ice cream, curd, dal, khichdi, mashed banana). Apply cold compress externally — 15 min on, 15 off. Sleep with head slightly elevated. From day 2, rinse gently with warm salt water 3–4 times daily. Rest for the remainder of the day.

✗ Don't (First 24 Hours)

Do NOT spit forcefully — this dislodges the blood clot. Do NOT use a straw — the suction pulls the clot out. Do NOT rinse your mouth vigorously. Do NOT eat hot food or drink hot tea/coffee. Do NOT smoke for at least 72 hours (smoking is the #1 cause of dry socket). Do NOT poke the extraction site with your tongue or finger. Do NOT do heavy exercise or lifting on extraction day.

⚠ Watch for Dry Socket

What it is: The blood clot at the extraction site dislodges, exposing bone. Causes severe throbbing pain 2–4 days after extraction. Signs: Intense pain that radiates to the ear, bad taste in mouth, visible whitish bone in the socket. What to do: Contact Dr. Mahima's immediately at +91 75977 47711. Dry socket is easily treated with a medicated dressing that provides near-instant relief. It occurs in roughly 2–5% of extractions and is more common with wisdom teeth and smokers.

Wisdom Tooth Removal — Do You Need It?

Not every wisdom tooth needs removal. Here's how to know if yours does.

Remove If:

It's causing recurring pain or swelling. It's growing sideways (impacted) and pushing against the next tooth. It's partially erupted and trapping food, causing repeated infection (pericoronitis). It has a cavity that's difficult to fill due to position. An X-ray shows it's likely to cause problems in the future (angled, no space).

Keep If:

It's fully erupted, straight, and positioned correctly. It's not causing any pain or infection. You can reach it with a toothbrush and keep it clean. It has a healthy opposing tooth to bite against. An X-ray shows it's not threatening the adjacent tooth. Many people keep their wisdom teeth for life without issues.
