Step-by-Step: Immediate Toothache Relief at Home

Follow these steps in order. They won't cure the problem, but they will reduce pain enough to get through the night until you can see a dentist.

Step 1: Take the Right Painkiller

Best choice: Ibuprofen 400mg (brand names: Brufen, Combiflam). It reduces both pain AND inflammation — which is why it works better than Paracetamol for toothache. If you can't take Ibuprofen (stomach ulcer, allergy, pregnancy), take Paracetamol 500mg (Crocin, Dolo 650). For severe pain, you can alternate both — Ibuprofen first, then Paracetamol after 3 hours, then Ibuprofen again after 3 more hours.

⚠ Never place an aspirin tablet directly on your gum — it causes a chemical burn that makes things worse.

Step 2: Warm Salt Water Rinse

Dissolve 1 teaspoon of regular table salt in a glass of lukewarm water (not hot). Swish it gently around the painful area for 30 seconds, then spit. Repeat 3–4 times. Salt water kills bacteria, reduces gum swelling, and draws out fluid from inflamed tissue. This is one of the few home remedies that dentists actually recommend.

Step 3: Elevate Your Head

Stack 2 pillows and sleep semi-upright. This is the single most effective positional change you can make. When you lie flat, blood rushes to your head, increasing pressure inside the inflamed tooth pulp. Elevating your head by 30–45 degrees reduces this blood flow and noticeably decreases throbbing pain.

Step 4: Cold Compress (Not Ice Directly)

Wrap ice or a frozen pea bag in a thin towel and hold it against the outside of your cheek over the painful area. Keep it on for 15 minutes, then off for 15 minutes. This constricts blood vessels, reduces swelling, and numbs the area. Never put ice directly inside your mouth on the tooth — the extreme cold on an exposed nerve will cause intense pain.

Step 5: Clove Oil (Optional but Effective)

If you have clove oil (laung ka tel) at home, soak a small cotton ball and place it directly on the painful tooth. Clove oil contains eugenol — a natural anesthetic that dentists have used for over a century. It numbs the area within 2–3 minutes. You can also gently chew a whole clove near the painful tooth. Available at any Kota medical store for ₹20–₹40.

Why Does Tooth Pain Get Worse at Night?

Understanding why your toothache intensifies at night can help you manage it better.

Blood Flow Increases When Lying Down

When you lie flat, blood pressure in your head increases. If your tooth nerve is inflamed (pulpitis), this extra blood flow increases pressure inside the rigid tooth structure, causing intense throbbing pain. This is why elevating your head with pillows helps.

Fewer Distractions at Night

During the day, work, conversations, and activities partially distract you from the pain. At night, in a quiet room, your brain focuses entirely on the pain signal. The pain hasn't actually increased — your awareness of it has.

Teeth Grinding (Bruxism)

Many people unconsciously clench or grind their teeth during sleep. If you already have a cracked tooth, cavity, or inflamed nerve, this grinding puts enormous pressure on the damaged tooth, triggering sharp pain that wakes you up.

Late-Night Eating Without Brushing

If you eat or drink (especially sugary foods or chai) before bed without brushing, food particles and sugar sit on the cavity all night. Bacteria feed on this sugar, produce acid, and irritate the already exposed nerve — leading to pain that starts 1–2 hours after you fall asleep.

When to Rush to a Dentist vs. Wait Till Morning

🚨 See a Dentist Immediately

Facial swelling spreading to eye, neck, or under the jaw. Fever above 100°F (38°C) with tooth pain — sign of spreading infection. Tooth knocked out or broken in an accident (within 30 minutes improves chances of saving it). Uncontrollable bleeding from the mouth. Pus discharge or foul taste — dental abscess. Pain so severe that maximum-dose painkillers provide no relief.

✓ Can Wait Till Morning

Mild to moderate pain that responds to Ibuprofen. Sensitivity to hot or cold without any swelling. A small chip in a tooth with no sharp edges cutting your tongue or cheek. Gum pain from food stuck between teeth (try flossing gently). Dull ache that comes and goes. In all these cases, follow the 5-step home relief guide above and call Dr. Mahima's in the morning for an appointment.
